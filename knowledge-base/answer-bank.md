### Why VC / why investing?
I've spent three years inside the full venture lifecycle — sourcing and diligencing deals at a $100M+ foundation fund, building four companies from zero, and running fund operations and LP reporting. What I'm best at is the judgment call: why a business matters, how the model wins, whether the numbers hold. Investing is where that judgment compounds. I want to move from running one company at a time to backing the people building the next frontier — and I've deliberately stacked the operator reps to do it well.

### Why hard tech / deep tech? (the thesis)
After years in B2B SaaS and AI applications, I became convinced that AI application software is increasingly un-investable — moats are thin, incumbents and fast-followers replicate features in weeks, and there's too much noise to differentiate or sell. The durable returns are in hard tech: physical AI, robotics, space, and the industrial stack. It's genuinely hard to build — more than a vibe-coded app — and it matters. U.S. reindustrialization is the tailwind. I spent the last year on it directly: ~100 interviews with founders and operators across energy, manufacturing, and industrials, and I got fluent in SBIR/STTR non-dilutive funding through OCAST and Oklahoma Catalyst. This isn't a pivot of convenience — it's where I've put my time.

### Walk me through a company / space you'd invest in (live thesis)
The space I'm most excited about is onshoring rapid-scale manufacturing. China's real strategic edge isn't cheap labor anymore — it's the ability to spin up a new physical product and scale it to volume in a fraction of the time the U.S. can. We don't have that muscle, and with reshoring and defense-production urgency, it's a massive gap. I'd back the picks-and-shovels layer: flexible automation, robotics, and software that compress the time from prototype to high-volume production. The thesis rides real tailwinds — reindustrialization, defense demand, supply-chain resilience — and non-dilutive capital (SBIR/STTR, defense programs) that de-risks the early rounds.

### A deal you'd pass on and why
At Atento I diligenced three companies and recommended against all three — they couldn't scale, the markets were too small, and the expansion stories weren't believable. All three are no longer operating. I'd rather be right than agreeable, and I'll do the work to defend a pass. More broadly, today I'd pass on most AI application software: thin moats, fast-follower risk, and crowded categories where the winners are already emerging. If a company's only edge is a wrapper on a model anyone can call, the demand and defensibility usually aren't there.

### Tell me about yourself / your story
I'm an operator who understands how companies actually work, and I get into the weeds to make them work. Three years ago I chose a venture studio on purpose — to learn the whole lifecycle fast. I built four companies from a blank page, took one (Agentech) to $600K ARR, a $3M seed, and a ~$25M valuation, where I'm now part-time CFO. I built the studio's fund operations and LP reporting from scratch on an $8M fund, and before that I sourced and diligenced deals at Atento, a $100M+ foundation fund. Along the way I got obsessive about hard tech and built production AI systems to do my own job better. I'm young by years but deep by reps — and ready to expand my ownership by backing founders, not just operating.

### Your edge / what you uniquely bring
Three things. First, business judgment — I can take a company apart end to end: the demand, the model, the market, and the financials underneath. Second, execution speed — if it needs to get done, I drive it to done at an uncomfortable pace and high accuracy. Third, and rare: I build production AI systems. At Agentech I built a multi-agent investor-sourcing engine with a learning loop, and a Claude platform that runs the sales team's briefs, meeting prep, and proposals across the full tool stack. Most investors talk about AI; I ship it. Together: an operator who can also pick — and who'll out-work and out-tool the room.

### Greatest accomplishment
Agentech. I took it from a blank page — a vague "something in vets" idea — through discovery, a pivot into pet-insurance claims automation, and on to AI-powered claims servicing. I built the team, coordinated engineering, product, design, and marketing, and ran sales myself to the first $600K in ARR. Then I raised its $3M seed end to end — memo, model, data room, investor pipeline — to a fast, fully-subscribed close. It's now around a $25M valuation and I'm its part-time CFO. It's the clearest proof I can do the whole job: judgment, build, sell, and fund.

### A weakness / growth area
My experience is compressed — three years, not ten — so I haven't yet sat through a full fund cycle as the decision-maker. I've offset it by deliberately taking the widest set of reps I could (sourcing, diligence, fund ops, building, fundraising) and by being honest about what I don't know and going deep fast. The flip side of my speed is that I sometimes have to consciously slow down to bring others along; I've learned to over-communicate the "why" so the pace doesn't lose the team.

### Where do you see yourself / why expand scope now?
I chose the studio to learn fast, and it worked — sourcing, diligence, fund ops, 0-to-1 building, fundraising. Now I want to concentrate that into investing: owning deals, building conviction, and backing founders in hard tech. I'm looking for a seat with real ownership and direct founder and partner contact, on a track that grows.
